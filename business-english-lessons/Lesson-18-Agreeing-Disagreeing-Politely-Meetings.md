# Lesson 18: Agreeing & Disagreeing Politely in Meetings (Advanced)

**Level:** B1+ (Upper Intermediate)  
**Students:** 8 adult learners  
**Duration:** 60–75 minutes  
**Focus:** Advanced phrases for expressing opinions, diplomatic disagreement, building on others' ideas, managing conflict

**Note:** This lesson builds on Lesson 12 with more advanced, nuanced language.

---

## **Stage 1: Warm-up & Lead-in (10 min)**

### Teacher Script:
"Welcome! Today we're taking your meeting participation skills to the next level. We learned basic agreeing and disagreeing in Lesson 12. Now we'll learn more advanced, diplomatic ways to express opinions, especially when you disagree or face conflict."

**Warm-up Questions (write on board):**
1. Have you ever been in a meeting where people strongly disagreed? What happened?
2. Is it easy or difficult for you to disagree with your boss or senior colleagues?
3. In your culture, how do people handle disagreement in meetings?
4. What's the difference between being polite and being too indirect?

**Instructions:**
"Discuss with your partner. 4 minutes. Share experiences of difficult meeting situations!"

*After 4 minutes:*
"Let's hear some stories. Who has experienced strong disagreement in a meeting?"

*Take 3-4 responses.*

---

## **Stage 2: Vocabulary/Context Brainstorm (5–7 min)**

### Teacher Script:
"I'm writing 'DIPLOMATIC DISAGREEMENT' on the board. This means disagreeing in a way that's firm but polite. In pairs, brainstorm: How can you disagree without offending? What phrases soften disagreement? 2 minutes!"

*Write "DIPLOMATIC DISAGREEMENT" on the board.*

*After 2 minutes collect vocabulary.*

*Expected: however, on the other hand, I appreciate your view but, with respect, alternative, perspective, consider, perhaps, suggestion, middle ground, compromise, etc.*

---

## **Stage 3: Listening Input (10–12 min)**

### Teacher Script:
"Now let's listen to a challenging meeting situation. There's strong disagreement, but people handle it professionally and diplomatically. I'll read it twice."

### **Listening Text (Teacher reads aloud):**

**Meeting: Budget Allocation Conflict**

**Chair (Anna):** Thank you everyone for coming. Today we need to decide how to allocate our $200,000 marketing budget for next year. I know we have different priorities, so let's hear all perspectives. David, you wanted to speak first?

**David (Sales Director):** Yes, thank you. I firmly believe we should invest 70% of the budget in digital advertising. Our online sales have grown 40% this year, and I'm confident we can double that with proper investment.

**Maria (Traditional Marketing Manager):** I hear what you're saying, David, and I appreciate the digital results. However, I'm concerned we're putting all our eggs in one basket. I'd like to propose a more balanced approach. While digital is important, we shouldn't completely abandon traditional marketing channels.

**David:** With respect, Maria, traditional marketing isn't delivering the results anymore. The ROI on digital is three times higher than print advertising.

**Maria:** I understand your point, and I don't dispute the digital ROI figures. But let me offer a different perspective. Our target demographic – people over 50 – still consume traditional media. If we cut traditional marketing entirely, we risk losing that segment.

**Chen (CEO):** That's an excellent point, Maria. David, what would you say to that?

**David:** Well, I take your point about the over-50 demographic. Perhaps I was too absolute in my recommendation. What if we allocated 60% to digital and kept 40% for traditional, specifically targeting that older demographic?

**Maria:** I appreciate you reconsidering, David. Though if I may push back slightly – 40% still seems on the low side for traditional. What about a 50-50 split?

**Lisa (CFO):** May I jump in here? I see merit in both arguments. Looking at the data, what if we took a phased approach? Start with David's 60-40 split, measure results quarterly, and adjust if needed?

**David:** I could support that approach. It's data-driven and flexible.

**Maria:** I'm not entirely convinced, but I'm willing to try it if we commit to genuinely reviewing and adjusting based on results.

**Chen:** I think Lisa's suggested a good middle ground. Before we finalize, does anyone have any other concerns or alternative suggestions?

**Tom (Product Manager):** Actually, yes. While I respect both marketing approaches, I'd like to respectfully challenge whether we're thinking about this the right way. Should we be asking 'digital vs. traditional' or should we be asking 'which channels reach our specific customer segments most effectively'?

**Maria:** Tom, that's actually a really insightful point. You're right – we're debating tactics when we should be focusing on strategy.

**David:** I have to admit, Tom's reframed this in a way I hadn't considered. Maybe we need to step back and segment our audience first.

**Chen:** Excellent discussion. I'm impressed with how we've handled different viewpoints professionally. Tom's raised an important strategic question. Let's take a 10-minute break, then come back and approach this from a customer-segment perspective.

### **Comprehension Questions:**

"Answer with your partner:"
1. What was the main disagreement?
2. How did Maria disagree with David politely?
3. Who suggested a compromise? What was it?
4. How did Tom reframe the discussion?
5. How did David show flexibility?

*Give 3 minutes.*

**Discussion:**
1. Was this a productive meeting? Why?
2. What made the disagreements professional rather than personal?
3. What phrases helped soften disagreement?

## **Stage 4: Language Focus (15 min)**

### Teacher Script:
"Excellent analysis! Now let's learn advanced diplomatic language for meetings."

### **Key Expressions:**

Write on board:

**ADVANCED AGREEING (Beyond "I agree"):**

**1. Strong Agreement with Added Value:**
- "I completely agree, and I'd like to add..."
- "Exactly! Building on that point..."
- "That's an excellent point, and it aligns with..."
- "I couldn't agree more, especially because..."

**2. Agreement with Evidence:**
- "The data supports your view..."
- "That's consistent with what we've seen in..."
- "Research shows that..."

**DIPLOMATIC DISAGREEMENT:**

**3. Soft Disagreement (Most Polite):**
- "I see where you're coming from, but I'm not entirely convinced that..."
- "That's one way to look at it. Another perspective might be..."
- "I appreciate your view, though I have some reservations about..."
- "While I understand your reasoning, I'm concerned that..."

**4. Moderate Disagreement:**
- "I respectfully disagree because..."
- "I have a different view on this. I think..."
- "I'd like to push back slightly on that point..."
- "With all due respect, I think we might be overlooking..."

**5. Strong but Professional Disagreement:**
- "I have to respectfully challenge that assumption."
- "I'm afraid I strongly disagree with that approach."
- "I must respectfully object to this proposal."
- "I cannot support this decision because..."

**OFFERING ALTERNATIVES:**

**6. Proposing Different Solutions:**
- "What if we approached this differently?"
- "Have we considered..."
- "An alternative might be..."
- "Let me propose a middle ground..."
- "Perhaps a compromise would be..."

**7. Reframing the Discussion:**
- "Maybe we're asking the wrong question."
- "I'd like to reframe this slightly..."
- "Can we step back and look at this from a different angle?"
- "Let's consider the broader context..."

**MANAGING CONFLICT:**

**8. Acknowledging Others' Points:**
- "You raise a valid concern..."
- "I take your point about..."
- "That's a fair observation..."
- "I hadn't considered that angle..."

**9. Showing Flexibility:**
- "I'm open to adjusting my position if..."
- "Perhaps I was too absolute in my statement..."
- "I'm willing to reconsider if..."
- "Maybe there's a way to incorporate both perspectives..."

**10. Seeking Common Ground:**
- "Where we agree is..."
- "I think we're aligned on the goal, just not the method..."
- "Can we find a solution that addresses both concerns?"
- "What if we tested both approaches?"

**ADVANCED MEETING PARTICIPATION:**

**11. Challenging Ideas (Not People):**
- ❌ "You're wrong about this."
- ✅ "I'd like to challenge that assumption."
- ❌ "That's a terrible idea."
- ✅ "I have concerns about that approach."

**12. Asking for Evidence:**
- "What data supports that conclusion?"
- "Can you walk me through your reasoning?"
- "What evidence do we have for this?"
- "How do we know this will work?"

### **The Disagreement Hierarchy:**

**Teacher Script:**
"Use these levels depending on the situation and relationship:

**Level 1 (Most Gentle):** "That's interesting. Another angle might be..."  
**Level 2:** "I see your point, but I'm not entirely convinced..."  
**Level 3:** "I have a different view on this..."  
**Level 4:** "I respectfully disagree because..."  
**Level 5 (Strongest):** "I must respectfully object to this proposal."

With your boss: Use Levels 1-2  
With colleagues: Use Levels 1-3  
On ethical issues: Use Levels 4-5"

### **Practice Dialogue:**

**Teacher Script:**
"Let's practice diplomatic disagreement:"

**A:** I think we should cut our customer service team by 30%.  
**B:** I understand the cost pressure, but I'm concerned that might damage customer satisfaction. What if we looked at other areas to reduce costs first?  
**A:** But customer service is our biggest expense!  
**B:** That's a fair point. However, it's also our biggest competitive advantage. Have we considered streamlining processes instead of cutting staff?  
**A:** I hadn't thought about process improvements. Tell me more.  
**B:** Well, if we automated routine inquiries, we might reduce costs without losing the personal service that differentiates us.  
**A:** That's actually an interesting alternative. Let's explore that.

"Practice in pairs, then create your own disagreement scenario!"

*Give 5 minutes.*

## **Stage 5: Guided Practice (10 min)**

### **Exercise 1: Make It More Diplomatic**

Rewrite these statements more diplomatically:

1. "That won't work."  
   → "I'm concerned that might not achieve our goals because..."

2. "You're wrong."  
   → "I have a different perspective on this..."

3. "That's a stupid idea."  
   → "I have some reservations about that approach..."

4. "We tried that before and it failed."  
   → "We experimented with something similar previously. Perhaps we could learn from that experience and adjust..."

**Students practice in pairs for 4 minutes.**

### **Exercise 2: Disagreement Scenarios**

"Your colleague says: 'We should stop all training programs to save money.' You disagree. What do you say?"

**Sample response:** "I understand the budget pressure, but I'm concerned that cutting training might hurt us long-term. Our skilled workforce is what makes us competitive. What if we looked at reducing external training costs by developing internal programs instead?"

*More scenarios for practice (3 minutes):*
- "We should start working weekends."
- "We don't need to invest in new technology."
- "Let's fire all temporary workers."

## **Stage 6: Communicative Task / Meeting Simulations (15 min)**

### Teacher Script:
"Now realistic practice! You'll have meetings with genuine disagreement. Show diplomatic skills!"

### **Meeting Scenarios (Groups of 4):**

**Scenario 1: Office Relocation Decision**

Your company is considering relocating the office. Group decides.

**Role A (CFO):** Strongly support moving to cheaper location (30% rent savings)  
**Role B (HR Manager):** Strongly oppose (long commute will make employees quit)  
**Role C (Operations Manager):** Concerned about moving costs and disruption  
**Role D (Sales Director):** Neutral, wants data before deciding  

Discuss and try to reach consensus. (6 minutes)

---

**Scenario 2: Product Launch Timing**

**Role A (Marketing):** Launch immediately (market opportunity now!)  
**Role B (Product):** Delay 2 months (product needs refinement)  
**Role C (Sales):** Launch now but limited release (test market)  
**Role D (CEO):** Want best decision for company (listen to all)  

Find a solution everyone can accept. (6 minutes)

*Groups meet, teacher monitors.*

"What decisions did you reach? How did you handle disagreement?"

*Debrief for 3 minutes.*

---

## **Stage 7: Wrap-up & Reflection (5 min)**

### Teacher Script:
"Excellent diplomatic skills! Remember:

**FIVE KEYS to Professional Disagreement:**

1. **Attack ideas, not people** – "I disagree with that approach" not "You're wrong"
2. **Use evidence** – "The data shows..." not "I think..."
3. **Show you're listening** – "I hear your concern about X, and..."
4. **Offer alternatives** – Don't just criticize, propose solutions
5. **Seek common ground** – "We both want X. The question is how..."

The best professionals can disagree strongly while maintaining respect.

What will you do differently in your next meeting? 30 seconds."
