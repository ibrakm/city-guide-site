# Lesson 19: Giving Feedback & Suggestions

**Level:** B1+ (Upper Intermediate)  
**Students:** 8 adult learners  
**Duration:** 60–75 minutes  
**Focus:** Constructive criticism, making proposals, positive and negative feedback, coaching language

---

## **Stage 1: Warm-up & Lead-in (10 min)**

### Teacher Script:
"Welcome! Today we're learning how to give feedback and make suggestions professionally. This is challenging – how do you tell someone they need to improve without demotivating them? How do you make suggestions without sounding bossy? Let's explore!"

**Warm-up Questions (write on board):**
1. Have you ever received feedback that helped you improve? What made it helpful?
2. Have you ever received feedback that made you feel bad? What was wrong with it?
3. Is it easy or difficult for you to give negative feedback?
4. In your culture, how do people typically give criticism?

**Instructions:**
"Discuss with your partner. 4 minutes. Share both positive and negative feedback experiences!"

*After 4 minutes, take 3-4 responses.*

---

## **Stage 2: Vocabulary/Context Brainstorm (5–7 min)**

### Teacher Script:
"I'm writing 'GIVING FEEDBACK' on the board. In pairs, brainstorm: What words and phrases are useful for giving feedback? Think about both positive and constructive feedback. 2 minutes!"

*Collect vocabulary: constructive, improve, strengthen, develop, suggestion, recommendation, observation, appreciate, effective, opportunity for growth, etc.*

---

## **Stage 3: Reading Input (10–12 min)**

### Teacher Script:
"Let's read about three different feedback conversations – one poor example and two good examples."

### **Reading Text:**

**Example 1: BAD Feedback (What NOT to Do)**

**Manager:** Tom, I need to talk to you about your presentation yesterday.

**Tom:** Oh, how was it?

**Manager:** Honestly? It was terrible. You weren't prepared, you spoke too fast, and nobody understood you. The client looked completely bored. You need to do better.

**Tom:** Oh... I thought it went okay...

**Manager:** Well, it didn't. Make sure this doesn't happen again.

**WHY THIS IS BAD:**
- Too negative and demotivating
- No specific examples
- No constructive suggestions for improvement
- Focuses only on problems, not solutions
- No acknowledgment of anything positive
- No opportunity for Tom to explain or discuss

---

**Example 2: GOOD Feedback (Balanced Approach)**

**Manager:** Tom, do you have a moment? I'd like to give you some feedback on yesterday's presentation.

**Tom:** Of course.

**Manager:** First, I want to say I appreciated your enthusiasm and the effort you put into the research. The content was solid and well-organized.

**Tom:** Thank you.

**Manager:** However, I did notice a couple of areas where we might improve for next time. Would you like to hear my observations?

**Tom:** Yes, please.

**Manager:** I noticed you were speaking quite quickly, especially in the first 10 minutes. I think some of the clients might have had difficulty following. Have you noticed that?

**Tom:** Actually, yes. I was nervous and I think I rushed.

**Manager:** That's completely understandable. Here's a suggestion: what if you built in more pauses? When you change topics, take a breath. It gives you a moment to calm down and gives the audience time to process. Would you be willing to try that?

**Tom:** That's a really helpful suggestion. I'll definitely try it.

**Manager:** Great. Also, I noticed the clients seemed unsure about the pricing section. Perhaps we could add more visual aids – charts or graphs – to make those numbers clearer? What do you think?

**Tom:** That makes sense. I'll work on that.

**Manager:** Excellent. Overall, Tom, you've got strong potential as a presenter. With these small adjustments, I think you'll be even more effective. Let's touch base after your next presentation and see how it goes.

**WHY THIS IS GOOD:**
- Starts with positive feedback
- Asks permission to give feedback
- Specific examples (not vague criticism)
- Offers concrete suggestions for improvement
- Invites discussion ("What do you think?")
- Ends on encouraging note
- Creates accountability (follow-up plan)

---

**Example 3: EXCELLENT Feedback (Coaching Approach)**

**Manager:** Sarah, I'd like to discuss the Johnson project. Do you have 20 minutes?

**Sarah:** Yes, of course.

**Manager:** First, I'd like to hear your perspective. How do you think the project went?

**Sarah:** Well, we finished on time and the client seems happy, so I think it went well overall. But I know there were some challenges with the budget.

**Manager:** That's a fair assessment. You're right – delivering on time was a real achievement given the complexity. Can you tell me more about the budget challenges?

**Sarah:** We went over budget by about 15%. Some unexpected costs came up that I hadn't anticipated in the initial planning.

**Manager:** I appreciate your honesty. What do you think you might do differently next time when it comes to budget planning?

**Sarah:** I think I should build in a larger contingency – maybe 20% instead of 10%.

**Manager:** That's exactly what I was thinking. In my experience, complex projects almost always have unexpected costs. Here's another suggestion: what if you consulted with someone from finance during the planning phase? They might spot potential cost issues earlier.

**Sarah:** That's a great idea. I hadn't thought of that.

**Manager:** I also wanted to highlight something you did really well – your communication with the client throughout the project was excellent. Even when problems arose, you kept them informed and managed their expectations. That's a real strength.

**Sarah:** Thank you, I appreciate that.

**Manager:** So, if I understand correctly, for the next project you'll increase your contingency buffer and involve finance earlier. Is that right?

**Sarah:** Yes, exactly.

**Manager:** Perfect. I'm confident you'll do even better next time. Let me know if you need any support.

**WHY THIS IS EXCELLENT:**
- Asks for self-assessment first
- Focuses on learning, not blame
- Uses questions to guide improvement ("What might you do differently?")
- Balances criticism with recognition of strengths
- Co-creates solutions rather than dictating them
- Confirms understanding and creates action plan
- Offers ongoing support

### **Comprehension & Discussion Questions:**

1. What's the main difference between Examples 1, 2, and 3?
2. In Example 2, how does the manager soften criticism?
3. In Example 3, why does the manager ask Sarah questions instead of just telling her what to do?
4. Which feedback style would work best for you? Why?

*Discuss 3 minutes.*

## **Stage 4: Language Focus (15 min)**

### Teacher Script:
"Now let's learn language for effective feedback and suggestions."

### **Key Expressions:**

**THE FEEDBACK SANDWICH:**

**1. Start with Positive:**
- "I really appreciated..."
- "You did an excellent job with..."
- "One thing that worked really well was..."
- "Your strength in this area is..."

**2. Give Constructive Feedback:**

**Softening phrases:**
- "One area for development might be..."
- "I noticed that... (observation, not judgment)"
- "Something to consider for next time..."
- "An opportunity for growth is..."
- "If I could offer one suggestion..."

**Being more direct (when appropriate):**
- "I need to address a concern about..."
- "We need to improve..."
- "This requires attention..."

**3. End with Encouragement:**
- "Overall, I'm confident you'll..."
- "I see great potential in..."
- "With these adjustments, I think you'll..."
- "I'm here to support you as you..."

**MAKING SUGGESTIONS:**

**4. Gentle Suggestions:**
- "What if you tried..."
- "Have you considered..."
- "It might be worth..."
- "One option could be..."
- "How about..."

**5. Stronger Suggestions:**
- "I'd recommend..."
- "I suggest we..."
- "My advice would be..."
- "I think we should..."

**6. Inviting Input:**
- "What do you think about..."
- "How would you feel about..."
- "Would you be open to..."
- "What are your thoughts on..."

**ASKING FOR FEEDBACK (Self-improvement):**

**7. Requesting Feedback:**
- "I'd appreciate your feedback on..."
- "Could you give me some feedback about..."
- "What could I do to improve..."
- "What's one thing I could do better?"
- "How can I be more effective at..."

**RESPONDING TO FEEDBACK:**

**8. Receiving Feedback Professionally:**
- "Thank you for that feedback."
- "I appreciate you pointing that out."
- "That's helpful – I hadn't thought of it that way."
- "Can you give me an example?"
- "What specifically would you suggest?"

**FEEDBACK BEST PRACTICES:**

**DO:**
- ✅ Be specific ("You spoke too fast in slides 5-7" not "Your presentation was bad")
- ✅ Focus on behavior, not personality ("The report was late" not "You're irresponsible")
- ✅ Offer solutions, not just problems
- ✅ Ask questions to encourage self-reflection
- ✅ Give feedback privately (especially negative)
- ✅ Give feedback soon after the event
- ✅ Balance positive and negative

**DON'T:**
- ❌ Give feedback when angry
- ❌ Compare people to others ("Why can't you be like John?")
- ❌ Use absolute words ("You always..." "You never...")
- ❌ Make it personal
- ❌ Give only negative feedback
- ❌ Give feedback publicly (unless purely positive)

### **Practice Dialogue:**

"Practice this feedback conversation:"

**A:** Maria, do you have a moment? I'd like to give you some feedback on this week's report.  
**B:** Of course!  
**A:** First, I want to say the data analysis was thorough and well-presented. I appreciated the charts – they made the information very clear.  
**B:** Thank you!  
**A:** One area I'd like to mention is the timing. The report was due Monday but arrived Wednesday. That created some challenges for my planning. Can you help me understand what happened?  
**B:** I apologize – I underestimated how long the analysis would take.  
**A:** I understand. For next time, what if you flagged early that you needed more time? That way we could adjust deadlines or get you support.  
**B:** That's a good suggestion. I'll do that.  
**A:** Great! Your work quality is strong, Maria. Let's just work on the communication around timing.

"Practice in pairs. Then create your own feedback scenario!"

*5 minutes practice.*

## **Stage 5: Guided Practice (10 min)**

### **Exercise 1: Improve the Feedback**

Make this feedback more constructive:

1. "Your work is sloppy."  
   → "I noticed several errors in sections 3 and 4 of the report. Let's review them together and discuss how we can ensure accuracy next time."

2. "You're always late."  
   → "I've noticed you've arrived 10-15 minutes late for the last three team meetings. This makes it difficult for us to start on time. Can we discuss what's preventing you from arriving on time?"

3. "That's a terrible idea."  
   → "I have some concerns about that approach. Can I share them? I'd also like to hear your reasoning."

**Students practice in pairs – 4 minutes.**

### **Exercise 2: Feedback Role-play Preparation**

"Prepare how you would give feedback in these situations:"

1. Your team member's emails to clients have spelling errors
2. A colleague speaks too quietly in meetings – nobody can hear them
3. Someone on your team did excellent work on a difficult project

*Students discuss and prepare – 3 minutes.*

## **Stage 6: Communicative Task / Feedback Scenarios (15 min)**

### Teacher Script:
"Now realistic feedback practice! Work in pairs. You'll practice both giving and receiving feedback."

### **Feedback Scenario Cards:**

**Scenario 1A: Manager**
Give feedback to your employee: Their presentations are informative but too long (scheduled 30 min, they took 45 min). This happened three times. Be constructive.

**Scenario 1B: Employee**
Receive feedback from your manager about presentations. Listen, ask questions, show willingness to improve.

---

**Scenario 2A: Project Leader**
Give feedback to team member: They contribute great ideas in meetings but sometimes interrupt others before they finish speaking. Several team members have mentioned this.

**Scenario 2B: Team Member**
Receive feedback about interrupting. You didn't realize you were doing this. Show openness to feedback.

---

**Scenario 3A: Colleague**
Your colleague asked for feedback on their report. The content is good but it's very difficult to read – no clear structure, huge paragraphs, no headings. Give helpful feedback.

**Scenario 3B: Colleague Requesting Feedback**
You asked for honest feedback on your report. Listen to criticism without getting defensive.

---

**Scenario 4A: Manager**
Give positive feedback to an employee who successfully led a difficult project. Be specific about what they did well. Also mention one small area for development (could improve time management).

**Scenario 4B: Employee**
Receive positive feedback from your manager. Accept it graciously and ask for specifics you can repeat.

*Students do 2-3 scenarios each – 12 minutes.*

"What was challenging about giving feedback? About receiving it?"

*Debrief – 3 minutes.*

---

## **Stage 7: Wrap-up & Reflection (5 min)**

### Teacher Script:
"Excellent feedback skills! Remember:

**THE FEEDBACK FORMULA:**

1. **Prepare** – Know specific examples
2. **Start positive** – Acknowledge what's working
3. **Be specific** – "You interrupted twice in slide 3" not "You're rude"
4. **Focus on behavior** – Not personality
5. **Suggest solutions** – "What if you..." "Have you considered..."
6. **Invite discussion** – "What do you think?"
7. **End encouraged** – "I'm confident you'll improve"

Good feedback is a gift – it helps people grow!

What's one thing you'll do differently when giving feedback? 30 seconds."

---

## **Stage 8: Homework (Optional)**

**Option 1 (Writing):** Write two feedback conversations (100-120 words each):
- One where you give constructive feedback
- One where you give positive feedback

**Option 2 (Practice):** Give someone real feedback this week (colleague, family member, friend). Use phrases from today. Write about the experience.

**Option 3 (Self-reflection):** Ask someone for feedback about your work. Write about: What did they say? How did you feel? What will you change?
