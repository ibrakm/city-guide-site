# Lesson 26: Polite but Firm Requests

**Level:** B2 (Upper Intermediate - Advanced)  
**Students:** 8 adult learners  
**Duration:** 60–75 minutes  
**Focus:** Being assertive while remaining professional, making non-negotiable requests politely, setting boundaries

---

## **Stage 1: Warm-up & Lead-in (10 min)**

### Teacher Script:
"Welcome to Module 4's integration lessons! Today we're focusing on a critical skill: being polite but firm. Sometimes in business, you need to be assertive – you can't accept 'no' as an answer – but you must remain professional and respectful. This balance is essential for effective business communication. Let's explore!"

**Warm-up Questions (write on board):**
1. Have you ever needed to insist on something at work? What happened?
2. Is it difficult for you to be firm with suppliers, colleagues, or customers?
3. What's the difference between being firm and being rude?
4. In your culture, how direct can you be in business situations?

**Instructions:**
"Discuss these questions with your partner. 4 minutes. Share experiences where you needed to be assertive!"

*After 4 minutes:*
"Let's hear from you! Who has struggled with being firm while staying polite?"

*Take 3-4 responses. Validate that this is challenging for many people.*

**Teacher adds:**
"Many people struggle with this balance. Some are too passive and get taken advantage of. Others are too aggressive and damage relationships. Today we'll learn to be assertive AND professional!"

## **Stage 3: Reading Input (10–12 min)**

### Teacher Script:
"Let's read about three different approaches to making firm requests – passive, aggressive, and assertive."

### **Reading Text:**

**Scenario: Your supplier is 3 weeks late with a critical delivery. You've already sent 2 polite reminder emails with no response.**

**Approach 1: PASSIVE (Too Weak)**

*Email:*
"Hi, I hope you're well. I'm so sorry to bother you again, but I was just wondering if maybe there might be any update on our order? I know you're probably very busy. If it's not too much trouble, could you possibly let me know when it might arrive? No rush though! Thanks so much!"

**Result:** Supplier doesn't take you seriously. Delivery delayed another 2 weeks. Your customers are angry.

**Why This Failed:**
- Too apologetic ("sorry to bother you")
- Weak language ("just wondering," "might," "possibly")
- No consequences stated
- No clear deadline
- "No rush" contradicts urgency

---

**Approach 2: AGGRESSIVE (Too Harsh)**

*Email:*
"This is completely unacceptable! You are 3 weeks late and you haven't even responded to my emails. This is terrible service and totally unprofessional. If you don't deliver immediately, I'm canceling everything and you'll never get business from us again. I'm also writing a negative review online."

**Result:** Supplier is defensive and offended. Relationship damaged. Problem still not solved because supplier loses motivation to help you.

**Why This Failed:**
- Attacking ("terrible," "unprofessional")
- Threats without constructive solution
- Burns bridges
- Emotional rather than professional
- Doesn't focus on problem-solving

---

**Approach 3: ASSERTIVE (Professional & Firm)**

*Email:*

"Subject: URGENT: Delivery Required by [Date] – Order #12345

Dear [Name],

We ordered 1,000 meters of fabric three weeks ago with an agreed delivery date of [date]. As of today, we have not received the shipment or an update on its status.

**Current Situation:**
- Original delivery date: [date] (3 weeks ago)
- Two follow-up emails sent: [dates] (no response)
- Our customers are waiting for their orders

**What We Need:**
I need confirmation of the delivery date by end of business today. The absolute latest we can accept delivery is [date – 3 days from now].

**If This Is Not Possible:**
If you cannot meet this deadline, please let me know immediately so we can:
1. Source the materials elsewhere
2. Discuss appropriate compensation for this delay

We value our business relationship, but this situation is critical for our operations. I need your response today.

Best regards,
[Your Name]
[Phone Number]"

**Result:** Supplier responds within 2 hours, apologizes, commits to delivery in 2 days, offers 10% discount. Problem solved, relationship maintained.

**Why This Worked:**
- Clear facts (not emotions)
- Specific requirements
- Firm deadline
- States consequences professionally
- Opens door to solutions
- Maintains respect while being firm
- Shows business impact (not just complaint)
- Provides alternatives

## **Stage 4: Language Focus (15 min)**

### Teacher Script:
"Excellent analysis! Now let's learn specific language for being polite but firm."

### **Key Expressions:**

Write on board:

**STATING NON-NEGOTIABLE REQUIREMENTS:**

**1. Making Clear Demands:**
- "I need [X] by [date/time]. This is non-negotiable."
- "I must insist that..."
- "This is essential for our operations."
- "I require [X] no later than [date]."
- "This deadline cannot be extended."

**Examples:**
- "I need the revised contract by Friday. This is non-negotiable."
- "I must insist that all team members attend this meeting."

**2. Stating What's Unacceptable:**
- "This situation is unacceptable."
- "I cannot accept anything later than [date]."
- "This does not meet our requirements."
- "We cannot proceed under these conditions."

**SETTING CLEAR BOUNDARIES:**

**3. Polite But Firm Refusal:**
- "I understand your position, but I cannot agree to that."
- "While I appreciate your suggestion, that doesn't work for us."
- "I respect your concerns, however we need..."
- "That's not something I can accommodate."

**Examples:**
- "I understand you need more time, but I cannot extend the deadline beyond Friday."
- "While I appreciate your offer, that doesn't meet our minimum requirements."

**4. Redirecting:**
- "What I can offer is..."
- "Here's what will work..."
- "The solution needs to be..."
- "I'm willing to discuss X, but Y is not negotiable."

**FOLLOWING UP FIRMLY:**

**5. When Promises Aren't Kept:**
- "I've requested this [X] times now."
- "This is the third time I'm asking for..."
- "As I stated in my previous email/call..."
- "We agreed on [date]. It's now [X] days overdue."

**6. Creating Urgency:**
- "This is now urgent."
- "Time is critical."
- "I need this resolved immediately."
- "We're facing serious consequences if..."

**7. Stating Consequences (Without Threats):**
- "If this isn't resolved by [date], I'll need to..."
- "Without confirmation by [time], we'll have to..."
- "This situation is affecting our [operations/customers/schedule]."
- "I'll need to escalate this to [person] if we can't resolve it today."

**ESCALATING PROFESSIONALLY:**

**8. Moving Up the Chain:**
- "I'd prefer to resolve this with you, but if that's not possible, I'll need to speak with your manager."
- "I've been working with you to solve this. At this point, I need to involve [senior person]."
- "Given the urgency, I'm copying [manager] on this email."

**9. Being Direct Without Being Rude:**
- "To be clear: we need X by Y."
- "Let me be direct about our requirements..."
- "I want to be upfront with you..."
- "Let me clarify our expectations..."

**MAINTAINING PROFESSIONALISM:**

**10. Firm But Respectful:**
- "I understand this is challenging, however..."
- "I appreciate your efforts, but the result must be..."
- "I value our relationship, which is why I'm being direct..."
- "With respect, this needs to be addressed immediately."

### **The Assertiveness Formula:**

**Teacher Script:**
"Use this structure for firm requests:

1. **State the facts** (what was agreed, what happened)
2. **State the impact** (how it affects you/your business)
3. **State what you need** (specific requirement)
4. **State the deadline** (non-negotiable timeline)
5. **State consequences** (what happens if not met)
6. **Offer to discuss** (but keep boundaries firm)

Example:
'We agreed on delivery by March 1st. [FACT]
It's now March 15th and we haven't received it. [FACT]
Our customers are waiting and we're losing sales. [IMPACT]
I need the shipment by March 20th at the latest. [REQUIREMENT + DEADLINE]
If that's not possible, I'll need to source elsewhere and discuss compensation for losses. [CONSEQUENCE]
Please confirm by end of day today. [ACTION]'"

### **Practice Dialogues:**

**Teacher Script:**
"Let's practice being polite but firm. I'll read Person A, you all read Person B."

**Scenario 1: Late Payment**

**You (Supplier):** Hi Marco, I'm calling about invoice #4532. It was due 30 days ago and remains unpaid.  
**Customer:** Oh, sorry, we've been busy. I'll get to it when I can.  
**You:** I understand you're busy. However, payment is now 30 days overdue. I need payment by Friday this week. If payment isn't received by then, I'll need to pause all future deliveries until the account is current.  
**Customer:** Okay, I understand. I'll process it this week.  
**You:** Thank you. I'll need confirmation of payment by Friday. I'll follow up if I haven't received it by then.

**Scenario 2: Scope Creep**

**Client:** Can you add these 5 new features to the project?  
**You:** I appreciate you want these features. However, they're outside the agreed project scope. Adding them would require additional time and budget. What I can do is complete the current project as agreed, then we can discuss a separate project for the new features. Or, we can revise the current contract to include them, but that will extend the timeline by 3 weeks and increase the cost by $5,000.  
**Client:** Hmm, I hoped you could just add them.  
**You:** I understand. However, the current agreement doesn't include them, and they represent significant additional work. Which option would you prefer: separate project or revised contract?

**Teacher Script:**
"Good! Now practice these scenarios in pairs. Then create your own situations where you need to be firm!"

*Give 5 minutes for practice.*

## **Stage 5: Guided Practice (10 min)**

### Teacher Script:
"Excellent! Now let's practice transforming weak or aggressive statements into assertive ones."

### **Exercise 1: Improve These Requests**

**Instructions:**
Rewrite these to be polite but firm:

**1. Passive:** "Um, if it's not too much trouble, could you maybe try to send the report sometime soon-ish?"

**Assertive Version:** "I need the report by Friday at 5 PM. Please confirm you can meet this deadline."

**2. Aggressive:** "You're always late! Send me that report now or you're in big trouble!"

**Assertive Version:** "The report was due yesterday and I haven't received it yet. This affects our client presentation tomorrow. I need it by 2 PM today. Can you commit to that deadline?"

**3. Passive:** "I'm really sorry to bother you, but our payment is sort of late. If you get a chance, maybe you could look into it? No worries if not!"

**Assertive Version:** "Our payment of $5,000 is now 45 days overdue. I need payment processed by Friday. If there's an issue preventing payment, please let me know today so we can resolve it."

**4. Aggressive:** "This is ridiculous! Your service is terrible! I demand a refund immediately!"

**Assertive Version:** "I'm not satisfied with the service I received. The product doesn't meet the specifications we agreed on. I'd like to discuss either a replacement or a refund. Please let me know how you'd like to resolve this."

**Students practice in pairs – 4 minutes.**

### **Exercise 2: What Would You Say?**

**Teacher Script:**
"How would you handle these situations? Create a polite but firm response."

**Scenario 1:** Your colleague keeps missing deadlines, affecting your work.

**Sample Response:** "Tom, I need to discuss the project deadlines. The last three deliverables from you were 2-3 days late, which affects my ability to meet my deadlines. I need your commitment that future deadlines will be met. If you're facing capacity issues, let's discuss that now so we can adjust expectations or get you support."

**Scenario 2:** A supplier wants to increase prices mid-contract.

**Sample Response:** "I understand costs have increased. However, we have a signed contract with agreed pricing through December. I cannot accept a price increase during the contract period. I'm willing to discuss new terms for the next contract period starting January, but current pricing must be honored."

**Scenario 3:** Your boss keeps adding tasks without removing anything from your workload.

**Sample Response:** "I want to be clear about my current workload. I'm currently working on A, B, and C. Adding project D would require me to either extend deadlines on current projects or work unsustainable hours. Can we discuss priorities? Which project should I delay or delegate to accommodate this new one?"

*Students discuss and create responses in pairs – 4 minutes.*

## **Stage 6: Communicative Task / Assertiveness Role-plays (15 min)**

### Teacher Script:
"Perfect! Now realistic practice! You'll face situations requiring assertive communication. Be polite but firm!"

### **Assertiveness Scenario Cards:**

**Scenario 1A: Buyer (You Need to Be Firm)**
Your supplier delivered 500 units. 100 are defective (20%!). This is the THIRD time this has happened. You've been patient before, but this is unacceptable. You need:
- Full replacement of all 100 defective units within 1 week
- 15% discount on this order as compensation
- Guarantee this won't happen again, or you'll find a new supplier

Be firm but professional. You value the relationship but can't continue like this.

**Scenario 1B: Supplier**
Your customer is complaining about 20% defective rate. This is embarrassing – quality control failed. You want to keep the customer but you can't afford 15% discount (your profit margin is only 12%). Offer solutions but protect your business.

---

**Scenario 2A: Project Manager (You Need to Be Firm)**
Your team member (Partner B) has missed the last 3 deadlines. Always has excuses. You've been understanding, but it's affecting the whole team and your own reputation with clients. You need to:
- Set clear expectation: deadlines MUST be met
- Understand if there's a genuine problem
- Make clear there will be consequences for future missed deadlines

Be supportive but firm – this can't continue.

**Scenario 2B: Team Member**
Your manager is confronting you about missed deadlines. Truth is, you're overwhelmed – you have too much work. You're not lazy, you're drowning. Explain your situation but also take responsibility.

---

**Scenario 3A: Client (You Need to Be Firm)**
You hired a designer for a website. Contract said 6 weeks, cost $10,000. It's now week 10, and they keep asking for more money ($3,000 more!). Work is only 60% complete. You need to:
- Insist they complete the work as contracted
- Refuse additional payment (unless scope changed)
- Set firm deadline: 2 weeks to complete or you'll hire someone else to finish and deduct costs from their payment

Be professional but protect your business.

**Scenario 3B: Designer**
You underestimated the work. Client keeps changing requirements ("just small tweaks"). You've already spent 120 hours (budgeted 80). You need more money or you'll lose money on this project. Negotiate while maintaining relationship.

---

**Scenario 4A: Employee (You Need to Set Boundaries)**
Your boss keeps calling you after work hours (8 PM, 10 PM, even weekends) about non-urgent issues. It's affecting your personal life and causing stress. You need to:
- Respectfully set boundaries
- Propose solution (you're available for genuine emergencies, but not routine matters after hours)
- Stay professional (boss is not malicious, just doesn't realize the impact)

Be respectful but firm – you have a right to work-life balance.

**Scenario 4B: Boss**
Your employee wants to talk to you about after-hours calls. You didn't realize you were calling too often – you're just excited about work and think of questions. You're not trying to be difficult. Listen and find a solution.

---

**Scenario 5A: Vendor (You Need Payment)**
Client owes you $15,000. Invoice is 60 days overdue. You've sent 3 polite reminders – no response. You're a small business – you NEED this money to pay YOUR suppliers. You must:
- Get commitment to payment
- Set clear deadline (this week)
- Be prepared to escalate (suspend service, late fees, legal action if necessary)

Be firm – this is your livelihood.

**Scenario 5B: Client**
Vendor is chasing payment. Truth: you have cash flow problems right now. You want to pay but need 30 more days. Ask for extension while maintaining relationship.

---

**Scenario 6A: Customer (You Need a Refund)**
You paid $2,000 for training that was supposed to help your business. The trainer was unprepared, content was basic (nothing new), and it didn't meet your expectations at all. Refund policy says "satisfaction guaranteed." You want your money back. Be firm but fair.

**Scenario 6B: Training Company**
Customer wants full refund. Your policy usually allows refunds only within 7 days (it's been 3 weeks). However, "satisfaction guaranteed" was in your marketing. Find a solution – maybe partial refund or alternative training?

### **Instructions:**

**Teacher Script:**
"Each pair: Do 2-3 different scenarios. Take your role cards. Read them carefully.

**Remember:**
- Be FIRM about your needs
- Stay PROFESSIONAL
- Use facts, not emotions
- State consequences without threatening
- Look for win-win where possible
- But don't compromise on non-negotiables

You have 12 minutes! Go!"

*Monitor and take notes on language use.*

*After 12 minutes:*

**Teacher Script:**
"Excellent assertiveness! Let's hear from one pair. Scenario 1, can you demonstrate your negotiation?"

*One pair demonstrates (2 minutes).*

"Great! What assertive language did you hear? How did they balance firmness with professionalism?"

## **Stage 7: Wrap-up & Reflection (5 min)**

### Teacher Script:
"Outstanding work! You've learned to be assertive while staying professional!

**THE ASSERTIVENESS GOLDEN RULES:**

1. **Use facts, not emotions** – "You promised delivery on the 15th" not "You always let me down!"

2. **State your needs clearly** – Don't hint, don't apologize excessively, be direct

3. **Set firm boundaries** – "I can do X, I cannot do Y"

4. **State consequences professionally** – "If X doesn't happen, then Y will occur"

5. **Don't apologize for reasonable requests** – Stop saying "Sorry to bother you" when it's legitimate!

6. **Follow through** – If you set a consequence, you must enforce it or you lose credibility

**Remember the difference:**

**Passive** = You get walked over  
**Aggressive** = You damage relationships  
**Assertive** = You get what you need AND maintain respect

**The key:** Firm on the issue, soft on the person.

Think: What situation in your work requires more assertiveness? What will you do differently? 30 seconds."

*Brief discussion.*

"Remember: Assertiveness is not about being difficult – it's about clear communication and mutual respect. You have the right to set boundaries and expect commitments to be honored!"

## **Teacher Notes:**

This lesson addresses a critical gap many business English learners face: being too polite/passive in English even when firmness is necessary.

**Cultural Sensitivity:** Some cultures value indirect communication. Acknowledge this but explain that in many business contexts (especially with Western partners), assertiveness is expected and respected.

**For Students Who Struggle:** Some students will find this very difficult. Encourage them that assertiveness is a skill that can be learned. Start small – practice in class before trying in real situations.

**Real Impact:** This lesson can genuinely change students' professional effectiveness. Many will report back that learning assertive communication improved their work relationships and results.
