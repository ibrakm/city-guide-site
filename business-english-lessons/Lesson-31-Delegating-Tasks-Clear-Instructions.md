# Lesson 31: Delegating Tasks & Giving Clear Instructions

**Level:** B2 (Upper Intermediate - Advanced)  
**Students:** 8 adult learners  
**Duration:** 60–75 minutes  
**Focus:** Effective delegation, clear instructions, assigning work, ensuring understanding, following up

---

## **Stage 1: Warm-up & Lead-in (10 min)**

### Teacher Script:
"Welcome to Module 5: Leadership & Management Communication! This module is designed for those moving into or already in management roles. Today we're focusing on delegation – one of the most important management skills. How do you assign work effectively? How do you ensure people understand what you need? Let's explore!"

**Warm-up Questions (write on board):**
1. Have you ever been in a management position? What was challenging about it?
2. Have you received an unclear instruction from a manager? What happened?
3. Why do some managers find it difficult to delegate?
4. What makes a good delegator?

**Instructions:**
"Discuss these questions with your partner. 4 minutes. Think about both giving and receiving instructions!"

*After 4 minutes:*
"Let's hear your experiences. Who has struggled with either giving or receiving unclear instructions?"

*Take 3-4 responses. Validate that delegation is a skill that must be learned.*

**Teacher adds:**
"Many new managers struggle with delegation. They either micromanage or give too little direction. Today we'll find the balance!"

## **Stage 3: Reading Input (10–12 min)**

### Teacher Script:
"Let's read about three different approaches to delegation – one poor and two effective."

### **Reading Text:**

**Example 1: POOR Delegation**

**Manager (rushed):** "Hey David, I need you to handle the Johnson account. Can you take care of it?"

**David:** "Uh, sure. What exactly do you need?"

**Manager:** "Just... you know, the usual stuff. It's pretty urgent. Thanks!"

*The manager walks away.*

**What Happened Next:**
- David wasn't sure what "the usual stuff" meant
- He didn't know the deadline
- He wasn't sure about his authority (Can he make decisions? Contact the client directly?)
- He spent 3 hours working on the wrong things
- The manager was frustrated when David didn't deliver what was needed
- David felt set up to fail

**Why This Failed:**
- No clear objectives or deliverables
- No deadline specified
- No context about importance or priority
- No indication of authority level
- Manager assumed David knew what to do
- No opportunity for questions

---

**Example 2: GOOD Delegation (Clear & Complete)**

**Manager:** "David, do you have a moment? I'd like to discuss delegating the Johnson account to you."

**David:** "Of course."

**Manager:** "Here's the situation: Johnson Manufacturing is one of our top clients. They've requested a proposal for expanding their service package. I need someone to lead this project, and I think you're ready for it.

**The task:** Create a service proposal including pricing, timeline, and deliverables. The proposal should address their three main needs: faster response times, dedicated account management, and quarterly reporting.

**The deadline:** I need the first draft by next Friday, October 20th, so we can review it together before the client meeting on the 25th.

**Your authority:** You can make decisions on service features and timeline. However, please check with me before committing to specific pricing – I need to ensure it aligns with our overall strategy.

**Resources:** You'll have access to our template proposals, and Sarah from the sales team can provide background on the client relationship. If you need anything else, let me know.

**Priority:** This is high priority – Johnson represents 15% of our revenue.

**Support:** Let's schedule a 30-minute check-in on Wednesday to see how you're progressing and address any questions.

Does this all make sense? Do you have any questions about the task or what I'm expecting?"

**David:** "Yes, that's clear. Just to confirm – you need the first draft by Friday the 20th, and I should focus on response times, account management, and reporting. I'll consult with you on final pricing but can make other decisions independently?"

**Manager:** "Exactly. I'm confident you'll do great work on this. Feel free to reach out if anything comes up before our Wednesday check-in."

**Why This Worked:**
- Clear task definition
- Context (why it matters)
- Specific deadline with buffer for review
- Clear authority boundaries
- Resources identified
- Priority level stated
- Follow-up scheduled
- Opportunity for questions
- Confirmation of understanding

---

**Example 3: EXCELLENT Delegation (Developmental Approach)**

**Manager:** "Lisa, I'd like to talk about giving you more leadership opportunities. Are you interested in taking on more responsibility?"

**Lisa:** "Absolutely!"

**Manager:** "Great. I'm planning to delegate our quarterly client satisfaction survey to you. Before I explain the details, can you tell me what you know about this process?"

**Lisa:** "We survey our top 50 clients every quarter about their satisfaction and gather feedback."

**Manager:** "Correct. And why do you think this is important for our business?"

**Lisa:** "It helps us identify problems before clients leave, and it gives us data to improve our services."

**Manager:** "Exactly. So here's what I'd like you to own: the entire process from start to finish – designing the survey, sending it out, analyzing results, and presenting findings to the leadership team.

I'm not going to tell you exactly how to do it because I want to develop your problem-solving skills. However, I will provide some parameters:

**Must-haves:**
- Survey must go out by the 15th of the month
- Must include our standard satisfaction questions plus any additional questions you think would be valuable
- Final presentation to leadership by the 30th

**Your authority:**
- You decide the survey format and additional questions
- You decide how to analyze and present the data
- You can consult with the IT team for technical support

**My role:**
- I'm available for guidance when you need it
- I want you to come to me with your proposed approach for approval before sending the survey
- We'll do a dry-run of your presentation before the actual leadership meeting

**Development opportunity:** This will give you experience in project management, data analysis, and executive presentations – all valuable skills for your career growth.

What questions do you have? And importantly, do you feel this is manageable alongside your current workload?"

**Lisa:** "I'm excited about this! I have a few questions about the presentation format..."

*(They discuss details)*

**Manager:** "Perfect. Let me know your initial plan by Thursday. I'm here to support you, but I'm confident you can handle this. This is your project to lead."

**Why This Is Excellent:**
- Positions delegation as development opportunity
- Checks understanding and commitment first
- Balances autonomy with support
- Clear about decision rights
- Appropriate challenge (stretches but doesn't overwhelm)
- Coaching approach (asks questions rather than just telling)
- Checks workload capacity
- Sets up checkpoints without micromanaging

### **Comprehension Questions:**

**Teacher Script:**
"Answer these questions with your partner:"

1. What did the poor delegation example lack?
2. In Example 2, what information did the manager provide?
3. How is Example 3 different from Example 2?
4. When would you use the approach in Example 2 vs. Example 3?
5. Which style would you prefer if you were the employee?

*Give 3 minutes for discussion.*

**Open-ended Discussion:**

3. How much information is "too much" when delegating?

*Take feedback from 2-3 pairs.*

## **Stage 4: Language Focus (15 min)**

### Teacher Script:
"Excellent analysis! Now let's learn language for effective delegation."

### **Key Expressions:**

Write on board:

**THE DELEGATION FRAMEWORK:**

**1. CONTEXT (Why This Matters):**
- "Here's the situation..."
- "The background is..."
- "This is important because..."
- "We're facing a challenge with..."
- "I'd like to delegate this to you because..."

**2. TASK DEFINITION (What Needs to Be Done):**
- "The task is to..."
- "I need you to..."
- "Your objective is to..."
- "The deliverable is..."
- "The end result should be..."

**Examples:**
- "The task is to create a comprehensive proposal."
- "I need you to analyze the sales data and identify trends."

**3. DEADLINE & TIMELINE:**
- "I need this by [date/time]."
- "The deadline is [date]."
- "We're working toward a [date] launch, so I need this completed by [date]."
- "I'd like a first draft by [date] and final version by [date]."

**4. AUTHORITY LEVEL (Decision Rights):**
- "You have full authority to..."
- "You can make decisions about X, but please check with me on Y."
- "You're empowered to..."
- "Please run it by me before you..."
- "You don't need my approval for..., but do consult me on..."

**Examples:**
- "You have full authority to contact suppliers directly."
- "You can make decisions about the format, but please check with me on the budget."

**5. RESOURCES & SUPPORT:**
- "You'll have access to..."
- "You can consult with [person/department]."
- "If you need [resource], let me know."
- "The budget for this is..."
- "I'm available if you need guidance on..."

**6. PRIORITY LEVEL:**
- "This is a high/medium/low priority."
- "This is urgent."
- "This is important but not time-sensitive."
- "This takes priority over..."

**7. CHECKING UNDERSTANDING:**
- "Does this make sense?"
- "Do you have any questions?"
- "Is anything unclear?"
- "Can you summarize what you're going to do?"
- "Tell me in your own words what you understand."

**8. EXPRESSING CONFIDENCE:**
- "I'm confident you can handle this."
- "I think you're ready for this challenge."
- "I trust your judgment on this."
- "I know you'll do excellent work."

**FOLLOW-UP & ACCOUNTABILITY:**

**9. Scheduling Check-ins:**
- "Let's schedule a check-in for [date]."
- "Please update me on progress by [day]."
- "I'd like to see a draft by [date]."
- "Let's touch base midway through."

**10. Opening Communication:**
- "Feel free to reach out if you have questions."
- "Don't hesitate to ask for help if you need it."
- "My door is always open for this."
- "Please flag any problems early."

**COMMON DELEGATION MISTAKES TO AVOID:**

**Teacher Script:**
"Don't make these mistakes:

❌ **Reverse Delegation:** Employee brings you their problems and you solve them  
✅ **Instead:** 'What solutions have you considered?'

❌ **Unclear Expectations:** 'Just handle it'  
✅ **Instead:** Specific objectives and deliverables

❌ **Micromanaging:** Checking every 2 hours  
✅ **Instead:** Scheduled check-ins with autonomy between

❌ **Delegating Without Authority:** 'Do this but you can't make any decisions'  
✅ **Instead:** Clear decision rights

❌ **No Follow-up:** Delegate and forget  
✅ **Instead:** Structured accountability"

### **Delegation Level Matrix:**

**Teacher Script:**
"Match your delegation style to the person's experience:

**Level 1 - New/Inexperienced:** Detailed instructions, frequent check-ins  
**Level 2 - Some Experience:** Clear objectives, moderate guidance  
**Level 3 - Experienced:** Outcomes only, minimal supervision  
**Level 4 - Expert:** Full autonomy, strategic goals only"

### **Practice Dialogue:**

**Teacher Script:**
"Let's practice effective delegation:"

**Manager:** Kim, do you have 10 minutes? I'd like to delegate the monthly sales report to you.  

**Employee:** Sure!  

**Manager:** Here's the context: Our leadership team relies on this report for strategic decisions, so accuracy is critical. The task is to compile sales data from all regions, analyze trends, and highlight any significant changes. I need this by the 25th of each month. You'll have access to our sales database, and you can ask Chen from the data team if you need help with the analysis. You have full authority to format the report however you think is most effective, but please use our standard template for consistency. This is a high-priority recurring task. Does this make sense? Any questions?  

**Employee:** Yes, that's clear. Just to confirm – you need it by the 25th, I should analyze trends and highlight changes, and I can decide on the presentation format as long as I use the template?  

**Manager:** Exactly. I'm confident you'll do great. Let's check in after your first report to see if you need any adjustments to the process.

"Practice in pairs, then create your own delegation scenario!"

*Give 5 minutes for practice.*

## **Stage 5: Guided Practice (10 min)**

### Teacher Script:
"Great! Now let's practice with exercises."

### **Exercise 1: Complete the Delegation**

**Instructions:**
Fill in the missing delegation components:

"Sarah, I need you to _______ [TASK]. This is important because _______ [CONTEXT]. I need it by _______ [DEADLINE]. You have authority to _______ [AUTHORITY], but please check with me on _______ [BOUNDARIES]. You can consult with _______ [RESOURCES] if needed. This is a _______ [PRIORITY] priority. Does this make sense?"

**Students create their own delegation using this framework – 3 minutes.**

### **Exercise 2: Improve Poor Delegation**

**Poor delegation:**
"Hey, can you deal with the customer complaint?"

**Improved:**
"Tom, we received a complaint from customer #4521 about late delivery. I need you to investigate what happened, contact the customer with an explanation and solution, and document the issue in our system. Please resolve this by end of day Thursday. You have authority to offer up to a 20% discount or free shipping on their next order. If the customer wants more than that, please escalate to me. This is high priority – they're a key account. Let me know once it's resolved. Does that give you what you need?"

**Students practice improving these poor delegations in pairs – 4 minutes:**
1. "Update the website."
2. "Talk to the supplier."
3. "Fix the problem."

### **Exercise 3: Delegation Decision Tree**

**Scenario:** You need to delegate the client presentation.

**Questions to consider:**
- Who has the skills?
- Who has the capacity?
- Who would benefit from the development opportunity?
- What authority do they need?
- What support will they require?

*Students discuss in pairs – 3 minutes.*

## **Stage 6: Communicative Task / Delegation Role-plays (15 min)**

### Teacher Script:
"Perfect! Now realistic delegation practice! You'll be managers delegating various tasks. Show all components of effective delegation!"

### **Delegation Scenario Cards:**

**Scenario 1A: Manager**
Delegate to your team member (Partner B):
- **Task:** Organize the annual company conference (200 people)
- **Deadline:** Conference is in 8 weeks, need plan in 2 weeks
- **Authority:** Can book venue and vendors up to $50,000, must approve anything over
- **Resources:** Budget of $75,000, help from admin team
- **Priority:** High – CEO will attend
- **Employee:** Has organized small events but never this large

Use effective delegation! (Include context, check understanding, offer support)

**Scenario 1B: Employee**
Receive delegation from your manager. Listen carefully, ask clarifying questions, confirm understanding.

---

**Scenario 2A: Manager**
Delegate to your experienced team member (Partner B):
- **Task:** Lead the new product launch project
- **Deadline:** 3-month project, milestone reviews monthly
- **Authority:** Full authority over marketing strategy, product positioning, budget allocation (within $200K total)
- **Resources:** Cross-functional team (marketing, sales, product)
- **Priority:** Critical – company's main initiative this quarter
- **Employee:** Very experienced, performed well on similar projects

Use developmental delegation approach! Give autonomy but structure accountability.

**Scenario 2B: Employee (Experienced)**
You're excited about this opportunity. Ask about authority, resources, and success metrics.

---

**Scenario 3A: Manager**
Delegate to a junior team member who made mistakes before:
- **Task:** Create weekly status reports for the team
- **Deadline:** Every Friday by 5 PM
- **Authority:** Can decide format, must include specific KPIs you'll specify
- **Resources:** Templates from previous reports, can ask senior team members for input
- **Priority:** Medium, but consistency is important
- **Employee:** Made mistakes in the past (missed deadlines, incomplete work)

Be clear, specific, and set up checkpoints without sounding distrustful.

**Scenario 3B: Employee (Has Made Mistakes)**
You want to do better. Ask questions to ensure you understand exactly what's needed.

---

**Scenario 4A: Manager**
Emergency delegation:
- **Task:** Handle urgent client issue (angry client, service failure)
- **Deadline:** Must contact client within 2 hours, resolve by end of day
- **Authority:** Can offer refund, replacement, or service credit
- **Resources:** Access to client history, can escalate to you if needed
- **Priority:** URGENT
- **Employee:** Competent but hasn't handled this type of crisis before

Delegate clearly but quickly. Provide support without micromanaging.

**Scenario 4B: Employee**
This is stressful and urgent. Make sure you understand exactly what to do and your authority to make decisions.

## **Stage 7: Wrap-up & Reflection (5 min)**

### Teacher Script:
"Outstanding work! You've learned the fundamentals of effective delegation!

**THE SIX COMPONENTS OF EFFECTIVE DELEGATION:**

1. **Context** – Why it matters
2. **Task** – What needs to be done (specific!)
3. **Deadline** – When it's due
4. **Authority** – Decision rights and boundaries
5. **Resources** – Support available
6. **Priority** – How urgent/important

**Plus:** Check understanding + Express confidence + Set up follow-up

**Remember these golden rules:**

✅ **Delegate outcomes, not methods** – Tell them what, not always how  
✅ **Match the task to the person** – Challenge but don't overwhelm  
✅ **Give authority with responsibility** – Can't do the job without decision rights  
✅ **Follow up without micromanaging** – Scheduled check-ins, not hovering  
✅ **Let people learn** – Some mistakes are valuable learning experiences

Think: If you're a manager (or will be), what's one delegation habit you'll develop? If you're not a manager, what delegation style do you want from YOUR manager? 30 seconds."

*Brief discussion.*

"Good delegation develops your team, frees your time for strategic work, and builds trust. Start practicing!"

## **Teacher Notes:**

This lesson is designed for students in or aspiring to management roles. The content can be adapted based on student experience:

- **For newer managers:** Spend more time on the framework and structured delegation
- **For experienced managers:** Focus more on developmental delegation and coaching approaches
- **For non-managers:** Frame it as "understanding what good delegation looks like" so they can advocate for clear direction

**Key Teaching Point:** Delegation is not dumping work – it's a leadership tool for team development and organizational efficiency.
