# Lesson 20: Time Management & Deadlines

**Level:** B1+ (Upper Intermediate)  
**Students:** 8 adult learners  
**Duration:** 60–75 minutes  
**Focus:** Discussing schedules, setting and negotiating deadlines, prioritizing, managing time pressure

---

## **Stage 1: Warm-up & Lead-in (10 min)**

### Teacher Script:
"Welcome! Today we're focusing on time management and deadlines – essential skills in any business! How do you talk about tight schedules? How do you negotiate deadlines when they're unrealistic? Let's explore!"

**Warm-up Questions (write on board):**
1. Are you good at managing your time? What helps you stay organized?
2. Have you ever missed a deadline? What happened?
3. How do you handle it when someone gives you an impossible deadline?
4. Do deadlines motivate you or stress you?

**Instructions:**
"Discuss with your partner. 4 minutes. Be honest about your time management!"

*After 4 minutes, take 3-4 responses.*

---

## **Stage 2: Vocabulary/Context Brainstorm (5–7 min)**

### Teacher Script:
"I'm writing 'TIME MANAGEMENT & DEADLINES' on the board. In pairs, brainstorm: What words and phrases do you use to talk about time, schedules, and priorities? 2 minutes!"

*Collect vocabulary: deadline, priority, urgent, schedule, time frame, postpone, extension, realistic, manageable, workload, deliverable, milestone, etc.*

## **Stage 3: Reading Input (10–12 min)**

### Teacher Script:
"Let's read about three different approaches to handling deadline pressure."

### **Reading Text:**

**Situation 1: The Unrealistic Deadline**

**Client Email:**
"Hi Thomas, we need the complete marketing campaign ready for launch on March 15th. That gives you two weeks. Please confirm you can deliver by then."

**Thomas's POOR Response:**
"Okay, I'll try my best."

**Result:** Thomas worked 60 hours that week, delivered late anyway (March 20th), quality was poor because he rushed, and the client was disappointed.

**What Thomas SHOULD Have Done:**

"Thank you for the project. I'm excited to work on this! I want to make sure we deliver excellent quality. Let me break down what's involved:

- Market research: 3-4 days
- Creative development: 5-6 days
- Client review and revisions: 2-3 days
- Final production: 2 days

Realistically, to deliver quality work, we'd need 3-4 weeks. I could deliver a preliminary version by March 15th for your feedback, with final delivery by March 29th. Would that work? Alternatively, if March 15th is absolutely fixed, we could prioritize the most critical components and phase the rest. What's most important to you?"

**Why This Is Better:**
- Shows professional analysis
- Offers alternatives
- Involves client in decision
- Manages expectations
- Protects quality and his own wellbeing

---

**Situation 2: Multiple Conflicting Deadlines**

Sarah has five projects all due the same week:
1. Johnson report (very important client)
2. Team meeting preparation (her boss asked for this)
3. Monthly sales analysis (routine but necessary)
4. New product presentation (important but flexible timing)
5. Budget forecast (due to finance department)

**What Sarah Did:**

1. **Prioritized:** Made a list by importance and urgency
2. **Negotiated:** Emailed finance: "I have several urgent priorities this week. Could I submit the budget forecast by Monday next week instead of Friday? I want to ensure it's accurate."
3. **Delegated:** Asked a colleague to help with the monthly sales analysis
4. **Rescheduled:** Moved the product presentation to the following week
5. **Communicated:** Updated her boss on her priorities and got confirmation on the plan

**Result:** Everything delivered on time with good quality, no burnout, and her reputation as a professional remained strong.

---

**Situation 3: When You're Running Late**

Alex realized on Tuesday that his Thursday deadline was impossible to meet. He had two choices:

**Bad Approach:** Say nothing, hope for the best, miss the deadline, surprise everyone.

**Professional Approach (What Alex Actually Did):**

Tuesday afternoon email:
"Hi Jennifer,

I'm writing regarding the market analysis due Thursday. I've completed about 60% of the work, but I've encountered more complexity in the data than anticipated. Based on my current progress, I believe I'll need until Monday to deliver a thorough, accurate analysis.

I apologize for not flagging this earlier. I underestimated the complexity on Friday when we discussed the timeline.

I can deliver a preliminary summary by Thursday if you need something for your Friday meeting, with the complete analysis following on Monday. Alternatively, if the Thursday deadline is flexible, I'd recommend waiting for the complete version on Monday.

Please let me know which approach you prefer.

Best regards,  
Alex"

**Jennifer's Response:**
"Alex, I appreciate you letting me know early. The preliminary summary Thursday would be helpful for the meeting. Please send the full version Monday. In future, please flag potential delays even earlier – as soon as you sense a problem. Thanks for your proactive communication."

**Why This Worked:**
- Early warning (not last-minute)
- Honest explanation (without excuses)
- Offered options
- Took responsibility
- Maintained professional relationship

### **Comprehension Questions:**

1. What was wrong with Thomas's initial response?
2. How did Sarah manage multiple deadlines?
3. Why was Alex's approach professional?
4. When should you tell someone you might miss a deadline?

*Discuss 3 minutes.*

## **Stage 4: Language Focus (15 min)**

### Teacher Script:
"Now let's learn language for managing time and deadlines professionally."

### **Key Expressions:**

**DISCUSSING DEADLINES:**

**1. Setting Deadlines:**
- "We need this by [date/time]."
- "The deadline for this is [date]."
- "Could you deliver this by [date]?"
- "When can you realistically complete this?"
- "What timeline would work for you?"

**2. Asking About Deadlines:**
- "When do you need this?"
- "What's the deadline?"
- "How urgent is this?"
- "Is there any flexibility on the timing?"
- "What's driving this timeline?"

**3. Confirming Your Understanding:**
- "Just to confirm – you need this by [date], correct?"
- "Let me make sure I understand the timeline..."
- "So the deliverables are due [date], is that right?"

**NEGOTIATING DEADLINES:**

**4. Requesting Extensions:**
- "Would it be possible to extend the deadline to [date]?"
- "I'm concerned about meeting the [date] deadline. Could we discuss adjusting it?"
- "Given the scope, I'd need until [date] to deliver quality work. Is that feasible?"
- "Could we push this back to [date]?"

**5. Explaining Why You Need More Time:**
- "The project is more complex than initially anticipated."
- "We've encountered unexpected challenges with..."
- "To maintain quality standards, we'd need..."
- "Given my current workload, I'd need..."

**6. Offering Alternatives:**
- "I could deliver a preliminary version by [date], with the final version on [date]."
- "We could prioritize the most critical components for [date] and complete the rest by [date]."
- "Would a phased delivery work – Part 1 by [date], Part 2 by [date]?"

**PRIORITIZING:**

**7. Discussing Priorities:**
- "What's the most urgent priority?"
- "How would you rank these in terms of importance?"
- "Which deadline is most critical?"
- "If I can only complete two of these this week, which should they be?"

**8. Explaining Your Priorities:**
- "My top priority this week is..."
- "I'm currently focused on..."
- "Given the urgency of X, I've prioritized that over Y."

**WARNING ABOUT DELAYS:**

**9. Early Warning:**
- "I wanted to flag a potential issue with the [date] deadline."
- "I'm concerned I might not meet the [date] deadline because..."
- "I need to alert you to a possible delay with..."
- "Could we discuss the timeline? I'm seeing some challenges."

**10. Explaining Delays:**
- "Unfortunately, due to [reason], we're running behind schedule."
- "We've hit an unexpected obstacle that's affecting our timeline."
- "The [issue] has caused a delay."

**11. Offering Solutions When Delayed:**
- "Here's what I propose to get us back on track..."
- "I can deliver [partial work] by the original deadline, with [remainder] by [new date]."
- "To meet the deadline, we could [alternative approach]."

**TIME EXPRESSIONS:**

**12. Talking About Time Frames:**
- "This will take approximately 3-4 days."
- "We're looking at a two-week timeline."
- "I can have this ready by end of day Friday."
- "This is doable within the time frame."
- "That's a very tight deadline."
- "That's unrealistic given the scope."

**USEFUL PHRASES:**

**13. Managing Expectations:**
- "To deliver quality work, we'd need..."
- "I want to ensure we do this right..."
- "Rather than rush and compromise quality..."
- "I'd prefer to underpromise and overdeliver."

### **Practice Dialogue:**

**Manager:** Can you finish this report by tomorrow?  
**Employee:** I want to make sure I deliver quality work. The report involves data from three departments. Let me check what's realistic. Could I get back to you in an hour with a timeline?  
*(One hour later)*  
**Employee:** I've reviewed what's needed. To do this thoroughly, I'd need until Thursday. However, if tomorrow is critical, I could provide a preliminary summary tomorrow with the full analysis Thursday. Which would work better for you?  
**Manager:** Thursday is fine. I appreciate you checking rather than just saying yes and then missing it.

"Practice in pairs. Create similar scenarios!"

*5 minutes practice.*

## **Stage 5: Guided Practice (10 min)**

### **Exercise 1: What Would You Say?**

**Scenario 1:** Your boss asks for a report tomorrow. You need 3 days realistically.  
**Your response:** "I want to deliver quality work. This report requires data analysis from multiple sources. Could I have until Thursday? Alternatively, I could provide a summary tomorrow with the full report Thursday. What works better?"

**Scenario 2:** You have 5 tasks due this week. You can only complete 3.  
**Your response:** "I want to ensure I'm prioritizing correctly. I have five deliverables this week, but realistically I can complete three. Could we discuss which are most urgent?"

**Scenario 3:** You realize on Tuesday you'll miss Thursday's deadline.  
**Your response:** "I need to flag a potential issue with Thursday's deadline. I've encountered unexpected complexity and I'm concerned about delivering quality work by then. Could we discuss options?"

*Students practice similar scenarios – 4 minutes.*

### **Exercise 2: Prioritization Exercise**

"You have these tasks. Prioritize them (1=most urgent) and explain your reasoning:

- Client presentation (deadline Friday, very important client)
- Routine monthly report (deadline Thursday)
- New project planning (no firm deadline)
- Team meeting preparation (meeting Wednesday)
- Email responses (accumulating)

How would you organize your week?"

*Pair discussion – 3 minutes.*

## **Stage 6: Communicative Task / Deadline Scenarios (15 min)**

### Teacher Script:
"Now realistic practice! You'll negotiate deadlines and manage time pressure."

### **Scenario Cards:**

**Scenario 1A: Manager (Urgent!)**
You need a market analysis by Monday for a Tuesday board meeting. Your analyst (your partner) usually takes 2 weeks for this type of work. It's Thursday now. Convince them to do it quickly!

**Scenario 1B: Analyst**
Your manager wants a market analysis by Monday (3 days). This usually takes you 2 weeks. You have other projects. Negotiate a realistic timeline or alternative solution.

---

**Scenario 2A: Project Manager**
You have 5 team members and 8 urgent tasks due next week. You need to assign priorities and deadlines. Discuss with your team (partner) which tasks are most critical.

**Scenario 2B: Team Member**
Your project manager is assigning tasks. You already have 3 deadlines next week. Be honest about your capacity and help prioritize.

---

**Scenario 3A: Client**
You commissioned a website redesign. The developer (partner) said it would take 4 weeks. It's been 5 weeks and it's not ready. You're frustrated. The launch is critical.

**Scenario 3B: Developer**
Your client's website is delayed (technical issues you didn't foresee). You need 1 more week. Explain the delay professionally and manage their expectations.

---

**Scenario 4A: Employee**
You have three projects all due Friday:
- Sales report (routine, can probably be done Monday instead)
- Client presentation (absolutely must be Friday)
- Budget analysis (your boss asked for it)

Discuss priorities with your manager (partner) and request to move one deadline.

**Scenario 4B: Manager**
Your employee has three Friday deadlines. Listen to their concerns. The client presentation is non-negotiable. The others might be flexible. Find a solution together.

*Students do 2 scenarios each – 12 minutes.*

"What strategies did you use to negotiate? What worked?"

*Debrief – 3 minutes.*

---

## **Stage 7: Wrap-up & Reflection (5 min)**

### Teacher Script:
"Excellent time management skills! Remember:

**FIVE KEYS to Managing Deadlines:**

1. **Be realistic** – Don't promise what you can't deliver
2. **Communicate early** – Flag problems immediately, not at the last minute
3. **Offer solutions** – Don't just say 'I can't,' say 'Here's what I can do...'
4. **Prioritize ruthlessly** – You can't do everything. Focus on what matters most
5. **Protect quality** – Better to deliver late with quality than on-time with poor work

Remember: Managing up (communicating with your boss) is a professional skill!

What's one thing you'll do differently with deadlines? 30 seconds."
